### Introduction
The football landscape has undergone significant changes in recent years, with the traditional centre-forward, or No 9, becoming an endangered species. The days of Andy Cole and Dwight Yorke's prolific partnership at Manchester United seem like a distant memory, with modern teams opting for more fluid and flexible attacking systems. But is the decline of the specialist striker a permanent trend, or is there still a place for the traditional centre-forward in the modern game?

### The Golden Era of Centre-Forwards
In the late 1990s and early 2000s, centre-forwards were the dominant force in football. Players like Cole, Yorke, and Jimmy Floyd Hasselbaink were the stars of the show, scoring goals for fun and leading their teams to success. The 1998-1999 season was a prime example of this, with Cole and Yorke forming a deadly partnership that yielded 53 goals between them and helped Manchester United win the treble.

### The Rise of Possession-Based Football
Fast forward to the present day, and the football landscape has changed dramatically. Possession-based football has become the norm, with teams focusing on dominating the ball and wearing down their opponents through sustained periods of pressure. This approach has led to a decrease in the influence of traditional centre-forwards, who are often seen as too static and one-dimensional for the modern game.

### The Decline of the Centre-Forward
Opta data reveals a sharp decline in the influence of centre-forwards in the Premier League. Two decades ago, strikers scored 41.6% of the league's goals, but this season, that figure stands at just 25.9%. The top scorers in the league are now more likely to be wingers, No 10s, or false 9s, rather than traditional centre-forwards.

### The Reasons Behind the Decline
So, what are the reasons behind the decline of the centre-forward? One possible explanation is the lack of specialist training for young strikers. With the emphasis on possession-based football, many teams are focusing on developing players who are comfortable on the ball and can play in a variety of positions. As a result, the art of traditional centre-forward play is being lost, with fewer and fewer players being taught the skills and techniques necessary to excel in this role.

### Is There Still a Place for the Traditional Centre-Forward?
Despite the decline of the centre-forward, there are still many who believe that there is a place for the traditional No 9 in the modern game. With the right training and development, a specialist striker can still be a highly effective player, providing a focal point for the team's attack and offering a physical presence that can unsettle opposing defenders.

### Conclusion
The decline of the traditional centre-forward is a trend that shows no signs of reversing, but that doesn't mean that the No 9 is a dying breed. With the right approach and training, a specialist striker can still be a valuable asset for any team. As the game continues to evolve, it will be interesting to see how teams adapt and whether the traditional centre-forward can make a comeback. One thing is certain, however: the days of Cole and Yorke's prolific partnership may be behind us, but the influence of the traditional centre-forward will never be forgotten.
