### Introduction
The World Cup is known for its unpredictability, with underdog victories being a hallmark of the tournament. With the biggest ever World Cup set to feature 48 teams, an increase from 32, the potential for upsets, shocks, and surprises is greater than ever. In this article, we will analyze the data behind classic underdog victories at the World Cup, from USA 1994 onwards, and explore what it could mean for this year's competition.

### A Brief History of Underdog Victories
The World Cup has a long history of underdog victories, with one of the most notable being the USA team's shock defeat of England in 1950. This upset was a major turning point in the tournament and set the tone for future underdog victories.

### Methodology
To analyze underdog victories at the World Cup, we started by looking at data from the launch of the Fifa men's world ranking system in 1993. We analyzed each World Cup match in which an underdog beat a higher-ranked team, along with the ranking disparity between the teams. The bigger the gap, the higher the 'upset score', and the larger the circle in the graphics.

### Key Findings
Our analysis revealed some interesting trends and patterns. We found that upsets are more likely to occur in the group stages of the tournament, where teams are still finding their feet. We also found that teams from smaller countries are more likely to pull off upsets, with teams like Senegal and Ghana causing major shocks in recent tournaments.

### Notable Upsets
Some of the most notable upsets in recent World Cup history include:
* Senegal's 1-0 victory over France in 2002
* USA's 2-0 victory over Spain in 2009 (although this was a Confederations Cup match, it is worth noting)
* Ghana's 2-0 victory over the Czech Republic in 2006
* South Korea's 2-1 victory over Italy in 2002

### What Does it Mean for This Year's Competition?
With the expanded tournament format, there are more opportunities for underdog victories. Teams from smaller countries will have more chances to compete against top-ranked teams, and the potential for upsets is greater than ever. Our analysis suggests that teams like Morocco, Serbia, and Nigeria could be dark horses in this year's competition.

### Conclusion
Underdog victories are a key part of the World Cup's appeal, and our analysis has shown that there are some interesting trends and patterns behind these upsets. With the biggest ever World Cup set to kick off, we can expect more shocks, surprises, and upsets than ever before. Whether you're a fan of a top-ranked team or an underdog, one thing is for sure - this year's World Cup is going to be exciting.
